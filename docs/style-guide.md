# Aidin Niavarani — Writing Style Guide

> For use by Relay and Scribe when writing on Aidin's behalf.
> Samples weighted: LinkedIn posts (especially Sample 3) > formal cover letters.

---

## Voice in One Sentence

Thoughtful practitioner. Reflects honestly, speaks plainly, connects work to life.

---

## Tone by Context

| Context | Tone | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn post | Candid, grounded, reflective | Personal stories welcome. No hype. |
| Outreach / cold email | Direct, warm, professional | Brief. No fluff. One ask. |
| Cover letter / formal | Structured, earnest | More formal than LinkedIn but still personal. Avoid stiffness. |
| Internal / team comms | Casual, collaborative | Not analyzed here — extrapolate from LinkedIn voice. |

---

## Sentence Structure

- Favors **short declarative sentences**, especially for emphasis. Single-line punches after longer context-setting.
- Uses **fragments intentionally**: "The code worked. Core infrastructure solid."
- Paragraph rhythm: long → short → long → short. Creates breathing room.
- Lists are common in LinkedIn posts. Bulleted lessons, numbered takeaways. Not overused.
- Formal writing (cover letters) uses longer sentences with subordinate clauses. This is older/less natural — avoid replicating unless explicitly writing a formal document.

---

## How He Opens

- **LinkedIn**: Drops into a scene or time marker. "January 2025: We went live..." / "Christmas Eve and I'm thinking about..."
- **Formal**: States intent directly. "I am writing this letter to express my intent and interest in..."
- **General rule**: He doesn't ease in with pleasantries. He starts with the point or the moment.

---

## How He Closes

- **LinkedIn**: Ends with gratitude, a call to reflect, or an open question to the audience. "What was your hardest technical leadership lesson this year?" / "Merry Christmas from a dad entrepreneur."
- **Formal**: Thanks, sign-off, credentials. "Thank you for your time. Regards, Aidin Niavarani, BSc."
- Closings feel earned — not tacked on. He earns the emotional landing by building to it.

---

## Voice Patterns to Replicate

- **Pairs of contrasts**: "Both humility and conviction matter" / "Real restaurants. Real people eating. Real livelihoods."
- **Honest admissions**: Surfaces failures, wrong calls, hard lessons. Never glosses. "What I got wrong: Pushed too hard for too long."
- **Time-based structure**: Uses before/after frames to show growth. Dates, periods, milestones.
- **Specific numbers**: "Incidents down 90%." "Millions in transactions." "Team of 10." Grounding, not bragging.
- **Self-awareness without false modesty**: "I was proud of that." Direct. Not performative humility.
- **Practical takeaways**: Lessons are concrete and reusable, not abstract. "You can add observability later. You can't retrofit trust in payments."

---

## Use of Personal Life (Family / Parenting)

- Parenting metaphors appear naturally, not forced. They illuminate leadership or growth.
- The kid / family shows up in LinkedIn posts to humanize the work context — not as sentiment bait.
- Pattern: state the personal observation, then apply it to the professional context. Keep it brief. One connection, not a whole essay.
- Never lead with the personal if it isn't doing work. It earns its place by being the lens through which a professional insight lands better.
- **Avoid**: making parenting the center of the post. It's a supporting element.

---

## Words and Phrases — Use

- "Turns out..."
- "That's the real win."
- "Still learning."
- "Grateful. Proud."
- "Real [noun]. Real [noun]." (tripling or pairing concrete nouns for emphasis)
- "What I got wrong:" / "What surprised me:" (honest reflection framing)
- "Your job isn't X. It's Y." (reframe structure)
- "You can't [X] while [Y]." (constraint framing)

---

## Words and Phrases — Avoid

- Buzzwords: "synergy," "leverage," "game-changer," "disruption," "thought leader"
- Corporate softening: "circle back," "touch base," "bandwidth," "move the needle"
- Hollow praise: "I'm so excited to share..." / "I'm thrilled to announce..."
- Overlong qualifications in casual writing ("It is important to note that...")
- Excessive hedging ("I believe that perhaps it might be...")

---

## Formality Calibration

- **LinkedIn**: conversational but intentional. Contractions are fine. Sentence fragments are fine.
- **Outreach emails**: professional but not stiff. No contractions in subject lines. One clear purpose per message.
- **Cover letters**: more formal structure required — but still grounded in specific experience, not generic claims.
- **General rule**: Aidin's natural register is "senior practitioner talking to a peer." Write to that level.

---

## What He Does Not Do

- Does not name-drop or credential-stack in casual writing.
- Does not moralize or lecture. Lessons are offered, not preached.
- Does not write long introductions. Gets to the substance fast.
- Does not use passive voice as a hedge. He owns his statements.
- Does not chase trends in language. The LinkedIn posts feel current but not trendy.

---

## Structural Template — LinkedIn Post

1. Hook: scene, date, or blunt statement (1-2 lines)
2. Context: what was happening, what was hard (short paragraphs or fragments)
3. Contrast or shift: the change, growth, or realization
4. Lessons: bulleted or short paragraphs — practical, named, honest
5. Close: personal reflection, gratitude, or open question
